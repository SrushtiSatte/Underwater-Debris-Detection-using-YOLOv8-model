Overview

Understanding the Problem and Our Solution

Our oceans, covering over 70% of the Earth's surface, are essential for sustaining life, regulating climate, and providing livelihoods for billions. Yet, they face a growing threat—marine pollution. Each year, millions of tons of waste, from persistent plastics to harmful chemicals, enter our seas, disrupting delicate ecosystems, endangering marine life, and impacting human communities. Plastics break down into microplastics, infiltrating food chains, while agricultural runoff and industrial discharge poison habitats and weaken biodiversity. This crisis extends beyond environmental damage, affecting climate balance, food security, and economies worldwide. Addressing marine pollution requires global collaboration, sustainable practices, and innovative solutions to safeguard the oceans for future generations.




Fig 1: Composition of Sea Water Debris



The composition of marine debris is diverse, reflecting a mix of human activities and environmental factors. A significant portion, often exceeding 60%, is plastic waste, including bottles, bags, fishing gear, and microplastics, which persist in the environment for centuries. Beyond plastics, marine debris includes metals, glass, rubber, textiles, and organic materials such as wood and plant matter. Industrial and construction materials, such as concrete and tar, also contribute to debris in coastal and offshore waters. While some debris is buoyant and visible on the surface, heavier items sink to the ocean floor, affecting benthic habitats. This varied composition poses complex challenges for cleanup efforts, as each type of debris requires different methods for detection, collection, and disposal. Understanding the composition of marine debris is crucial for devising effective strategies to mitigate its impact on marine ecosystems.

Traditional Methods

1. Scuba Cleaning


Scuba diving involves a diver wearing a self-contained underwater breathing apparatus (SCUBA) that provides oxygen. This allows divers to explore underwater environments, including the ocean floor, lakes, and rivers. They use a variety of tools, such as underwater cameras, flashlights, and specialized knives, to navigate and document their findings. By physically inspecting underwater areas, divers can identify and remove marine debris, such as plastic bags, bottles, fishing nets, and other harmful materials.
Limitations of Scuba Diving for Underwater Debris Detection
1. Depth and Time Constraints
2. Human Error and Fatigue
3. Poor Visibility and Water Conditions





2. Underwater Debris Detection Using Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs)

Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs) are unmanned underwater robots controlled from the surface. Equipped with high-resolution cameras and robotic manipulators, ROVs are deployed to explore underwater areas. These vehicles are used for collecting debris and assessing underwater environments in real time. Operators remotely control the ROVs, utilizing live video feeds and sensor data to guide their movements and perform tasks such as picking up debris.

Limitations of Using Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs):
1. Require skilled operators.
2. High deployment and maintenance costs.
3. Limited range due to cable length.
4. Restricted operational time due to battery life.

Solution


Autonomous Robot

Fig 3: System Architecture


The underwater debris detection system is built around the powerful NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX computing module, which serves as its brain. This central processor integrates a 6-core ARM CPU for system management and a 384-core Volta GPU specifically designed for AI processing, supported by 8GB LPDDR4 RAM for swift data handling. The imaging system utilizes a high-performance 4K underwater camera (Teledyne FLIR BlackFly S USB3) capable of capturing 60 frames per second with a 120-degree wide-angle view and operational capability up to 200 meters depth. This hardware configuration processes data through a YOLO model for real-time debris detection and classification, all while consuming only 10-15 watts of power. The entire system is protected by an IP68-rated waterproof enclosure, which houses additional environmental sensors and thermal management systems to ensure reliable operation in challenging underwater conditions. This integrated setup enables efficient real-time detection of underwater debris while maintaining high accuracy through its machine learning capabilities, making it ideal for marine cleanup and monitoring applications. The system's robust design and advanced processing power allow it to operate effectively in varying underwater conditions while providing reliable debris detection and classification results.



Flowchart

Fig 4: Flowchart


The flowchart represents the process of underwater debris detection and management using a machine learning-based system. It begins with Acquisition, where underwater images or videos are captured using devices such as cameras or drones. The next stage is Image Pre-processing, which involves preparing the captured images by performing noise reduction to remove distortions, color correction to enhance underwater visibility, resizing the images to a fixed input size, and extracting key features like edges or textures for better analysis. After pre-processing, the images are passed through the Object Detection stage, where debris is identified and localized within the image. This step also involves classifying the type of debris based on its features.
The results from object detection are then fed into the Decision Making module, where the system interprets the detected debris, identifies its type, and determines the appropriate response. Following this, the system initiates Data Transmission, where information such as debris type and location is sent to the relevant monitoring systems or operators. Finally, in the Action Triggering phase, the system executes the required actions, which may include alerting an operator for manual intervention or activating a robotic arm to automate the removal of debris. This streamlined process integrates image processing, machine learning, and robotic systems to enable efficient underwater debris detection and management.
